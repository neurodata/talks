Those who would like to attend remotely can do so by watching the live stream of the Thursday morning session (available here, log in as a guest). Enable the audio feed by dialing into the appropriate country-specific phone number and entering the participant code: 7191250. You may use the Twitter hashtag #globalbrain16 for live comments.

Who will be on site?

Due to space constraints, we cannot accommodate all the neuroscientists in the world on site. However, we strove for adequate representation by inviting scientists from across all continents, from all subsets of the brain sciences, and who have very diverse skills and interests. Their backgrounds range from academia, government, foundations, and industry. This includes representatives from all of the national, transnational, and private neuroscience initiatives, including the US BRAIN project, the EU Human Brain Project, Japan Brain/MINDS, the Korea Brain Project, the China Brain Science Project, Brain Canada, Taiwan, and Australia, NSF, Kavli, the Allen Institute for Brain Science, HHMI, Amazon, Google, DARPA, IARPA, DoE, and the White House.

How to prepare?

All participants are invited to prepare a 1-minute elevator pitch of their best global brain project idea. We will try to incorporate as many remote suggestions as possible. Please email us your proposal no later than 7:00AM EST Thursday, April 7th, so that we can plan accordingly.

What was the motivation?

The international community has fortuitously adopted brain sciences as a key scientific topic for the coming decade. This has resulted in a number of national, transnational, and private commitments of literally billions of dollars (USD) over the next several years. To date, however, these brain projects have operated in relative isolation. An opportunity has arisen to unify portions of these efforts into an international one; we will be presenting our vision at the UN General Assembly in September. At the Global Brain Workshop, we will begin to create that vision together. The timing is perfect as we prepare for changes in leadership across nations, as well as a new UN General Secretary being selected this year.

What is the goal of the workshop?

The goal is to generate a set of potential pan-national brain science projects that will inspire international collaboration. Projects should be ambitious (i.e. worthy of a global decade-long effort), yet feasible and publicly responsible, on par with the International Space Station and the Large Hadron Collider. The workshop will produce an open access perspective piece containing 5-10 promising projects. For each project, we will address the following:

  1. Problem
  2. Background
  3. Gap
  4. Approach
  5. Roadmap
  6. Significance
  7. Feasibility
  8. Inclusivity

How will we do it?

We requested researchers to suggest projects from across the brain sciences. The suggested projects were then organized into seven different topic areas, but will be re-organized on site as needed:

  1. Synapse-Resolution Neuroscience
  2. Cellular Neuroanatomy, Neurogenetics, and Neurophysiology
  3. Crossing the Spatiotemporal Scales
  4. Learning & Development
  5. Computational Tools
  6. Theory & Modeling
  7. Medicine/Disease
  8. Mechanisms of Cognition and Behavior
The workshop will begin with each attendee (and some remote guests) delivering hir 1-minute elevator pitch. Then we will discuss and fuse these ideas into 5-10 visions, in a series of round-robin break-out sessions, each with a group mediator and rapporteur. For more details, see below
