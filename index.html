Who will be on site?

Do to space constraints, not all neuroscientists in the world could be local. Rather, we endeavored to include representatives spanning all subsets of brain sciences, including all continents, and ranging from academia, government, the private sector, and industry, with very diverse skills and interests. This includes representatives from all of the national, transnational, and private neuroscience initiatives, including the US BRAIN, the EU Human Brain Project, Japan Brain/MINDS, Korea Brain Project, China Brain Science Project, Brain Canada, Taiwan, and Australia, NSF, Kavli, Allen Institute for Brain Science, and HHMI, as well as Amazon, Google, DARPA, IARPA, DoE, and the White House.

How to Prepare?

All participants are invited to prepare a 1-minute elevator pitch of your best global brain project idea. We will try to incorporate as many remote suggestions as possible. Please email us your proposal sometime before the event begins so that we can plan accordingly.

What was the Motivation?

The international community has fortuitously adopted brain sciences as a key scientific topic for the coming decade. This has resulted in a number of national, transnational, and private commitments of literally billions of dollars (USD) over the next several years. To date, however, these brain projects have operated in relative isolation. An opportunity has arisen to unify portions of these efforts into an international one; we will be presenting our vision at the UN General Assembly in September. On April 7 & 8, the sixty-six of us begin to create that vision together. The timing is perfect as we prepare for changes in leadership across nations, as well as a new UN General Secretary being selected this year.

How Will We Do it?

We requested suggested projects from across the neurosciences, and organized the results into seven different topic areas, to be re-organized on site as appropriate:

  1. Synapse-Resolution Neuroscience
  2. Cellular Neuroanatomy, Neurogenetics, and Neurophysiology
  3. Crossing the Spatiotemporal Scales
  4. Learning & Development
  5. Computational Tools
  6. Theory & Modeling
  7. Medicine/Disease
The workshop will begin with each attendee (and some remote guests) delivering hir 1-minute elevator pitch. Then we will discuss and fuse the ideas in a series of round-robin break-out sessions, each with a group mediator. For more details, see below

Goal of Workshop?

The goal is to generate a set of potential pan-national brain science projects that will inspire and benefit from international collaboration. Projects should be ambitious (worthy of a global decade-long effort!) yet feasible and publicly responsible, on par with the International Space Station and the Large Hadron Collider. The workshop will produce an open access perspective piece containing a set of 5-10 promising projects. For each project, we will answer these questions:

  1. Problem Statement: what is the problem we are going to solve in the next 10 years?
  2. Approach: how will we solve the problem, how is our approach different from the status quo?
  3. Significance: what difference will it make, to which citizens of the world?
  4. Feasibility: why will it work, why might it not?
  5. Milestones & Deliverables: how and when will success be measured/quantified?
